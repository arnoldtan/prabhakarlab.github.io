Overview of Single-Cell In Situ Spatial Omics at subcellular Resolution(SCISSOR)

The global in vitro diagnostics (IVD) market is projected to reach USD120B by 2025 (Frost & Sullivan, 2016). The most precise IVD methods involve imaging or molecular profiling of blood, urine or other human tissue samples. In particular, the nucleic acid-based diagnostics market is projected at USD 24 billion by 2022, with a CAGR of 14.3% (BCC Research, 2017). A related market is that for molecular reagents and kits, which is estimated at USD 14 billion by the same date (Markets and Markets™, 2017).

Existing technologies in these market spaces are limited or lacking in one or more of the following capabilities:
1) Spatial Information
2) Single-Cell resolution
3) Number of Molecular Features that can be probed
4) Sensitivity

We aim to overcome these limitations by extending our unique spatial omics (SO) technology, Multiplexed Error-Robust Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization (MERFISH) Co-invented by Dr. Kok Hao Chen: Kok Hao Chen, Chen et. al. Science 2015.

Advantages of MERFISH

This multiplexed FISH approach provides four major advantages over existing methods: 1) spatial locations of nucleic acid molecules in the tumor sample 2) sub-cellular resolution 3) parallelization to more than 1,000 markers and 4) greater sensitivity and accuracy than competing methods. These advantages could provide greater speed, precision and convenience at lower cost for clinical diagnoses.

Why be Interested in SCISSOR?

SCISSOR is underway developing applications for spatial omics in three major areas of in vitro diagnostics:
a) Liquid Biopsy
b) Fusion Gene Detection in Sarcomas
c) Immunotherapy.

For each of the above application SCISSOR aims to develop:
1) Advanced Spatial Omics (SO) techniques and analytics
(2) Reagent kits
(3) Fee-For-Service collaboration

Our programme also promises to enhance existing practices in healthcare industry to impact lives, as current pathology methods are mostly low-throughput (at most 6-8 markers are profiled by Immunohistochemistry) and most oncologic diagnoses are still based on immunohistochemical analysis of tissue biopsies. Use of single-cell 3D transcriptomics-based decision support systems could increase information content from routine FFPE clinical material by many orders of magnitude, revealing cell-type-specific features and inter-cellular interactions that precisely define cancer biology and inform treatment. This enhanced diagnostic capability is particularly important as modern cancer drug development targets specific stromal, immune and cancer cell traits. Other applications are also promising – the technology is applicable in principle to any disease for which biopsy samples are accessible.
